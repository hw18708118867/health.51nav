## Why TDEE matters

If you want to lose fat, maintain weight, or gain muscle, TDEE is one of the most useful numbers to understand. It estimates how many calories your body burns in a typical day after resting metabolism, movement, exercise, and digestion are all counted.

What makes TDEE helpful is not that it is perfect. It is that it gives you a place to begin. Most people do not need an exact metabolic reading. They need a reasonable estimate they can test and adjust.

## TDEE starts with BMR

Most TDEE calculators begin by estimating Basal Metabolic Rate. The most commonly used formulas include:

- Mifflin-St Jeor
- Harris-Benedict
- Katch-McArdle

For general use, Mifflin-St Jeor is often the most practical because it performs well for many adults and only requires age, sex, height, and weight.

## Activity multipliers are where estimates drift

After BMR is calculated, it gets multiplied by an activity factor. This is the step where many calorie plans go wrong.

People often select an activity level based on workouts alone, but daily lifestyle matters too. Someone who trains four times per week but sits all day may still be closer to lightly active than they expect.

## Which TDEE formula is best?

There is no universal winner for every body. The best answer is usually:

1. Start with a practical equation like Mifflin-St Jeor
2. Apply a conservative activity multiplier
3. Adjust using real-world results over two to four weeks

That last step matters more than chasing theoretical precision.

## How to test whether your TDEE estimate is right

Track your daily calorie intake and monitor your average morning body weight for at least two weeks.

- If weight stays stable, your intake is close to maintenance
- If weight trends down, you are in a deficit
- If weight trends up, you are in a surplus

This turns your estimated TDEE into a personalized one.

## Final takeaway

The best TDEE formula is the one that gets you close enough to start and simple enough to adjust.
