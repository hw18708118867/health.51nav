## The right calorie target depends on the goal

When people ask how many calories they should eat, they are usually trying to answer a more specific question: how much should I eat to maintain my weight, lose fat, or gain muscle? The answer changes with body size, age, sex, activity level, and the goal you have right now.

## Start with maintenance calories

Maintenance calories represent the amount you eat while body weight stays relatively stable. This estimate usually comes from a [TDEE Calculator](/tdee-calculator), which combines resting metabolism with activity level.

Once you know maintenance calories, you can adjust from there:

- Eat around maintenance to stay near your current weight
- Eat below maintenance to lose fat
- Eat above maintenance to gain weight

## How large should the adjustment be?

Most people do not need an aggressive change.

- A moderate deficit of 300 to 500 calories often supports steady fat loss
- A modest surplus of 150 to 300 calories is often enough for leaner weight gain

Larger changes may look appealing on paper, but they are usually harder to sustain and less precise in practice.

## Calories are a starting point, not a verdict

Even the best calculator gives you an estimate. Water shifts, food-label error, exercise variation, and daily movement all affect the real answer.

That is why the best calorie target is the one you actually test. A number on paper is useful, but the pattern in your body weight, hunger, energy, and training tells you whether that number is close enough.
