## Why people still use BMI

BMI is still widely used because it is fast, simple, and easy to compare from one person to the next. In a few seconds, it gives a rough picture of body size relative to height. That is why it still shows up in clinics, research, insurance forms, and health websites.

The problem is not that BMI is worthless. The problem is that many people expect one number to explain everything. BMI can point to a broad weight category, but it does not directly measure body fat, muscle mass, fitness level, or eating habits.

## The BMI formula explained

BMI is calculated by dividing weight by height squared.

- In metric units: weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared
- In imperial units: weight in pounds divided by height in inches squared, multiplied by 703

Because height is squared, even a small entry mistake can shift the final result more than people expect. That is why accurate input matters.

## Common BMI calculation mistakes

### Mixing metric and imperial inputs

One of the most common mistakes is entering pounds where kilograms are expected or inches where centimeters are expected. This creates a completely misleading result. A calculator that lets the user switch between metric and imperial inputs reduces that risk.

### Rounding too aggressively

If you estimate your height or weight too loosely, you may end up crossing a category threshold without realizing it. That matters if you are close to 18.5, 25, or 30.

### Ignoring context

A BMI of 26 means something different for a strength athlete than it does for a sedentary adult with low muscle mass. The number is only one part of the story.

## What BMI categories mean

The standard adult categories are typically:

- Underweight: under 18.5
- Healthy weight: 18.5 to 24.9
- Overweight: 25 to 29.9
- Obesity: 30 or more

These categories are screening categories, not complete health verdicts. They are meant to flag whether deeper evaluation may be useful.

## When BMI is helpful

BMI works best when you want a fast starting point. It is useful for:

- General health screening
- Comparing your current result with public-health ranges
- Tracking whether weight change is moving you closer to or farther from a common healthy range
- Pairing with other tools like calorie, BMR, and TDEE calculators

## When BMI is less helpful

BMI becomes less informative when body composition differs a lot from average. This often includes:

- Athletes with high muscle mass
- Older adults with low muscle mass
- People in active body recomposition phases
- Pregnancy contexts

## A smarter way to use BMI

The best way to use BMI is as a first check, not a final judgment. Start with BMI, then look at other clues such as body fat percentage, activity level, blood pressure, and how your weight has changed over time. If your BMI is outside the common range, that does not automatically mean something is wrong. It usually means the next question matters more than the first number.
