## Why due dates are estimates

Pregnancy timing is usually measured from the first day of the last menstrual period, not from the day conception actually happened. That is why a due date is best understood as an estimate built around an average timeline, not a guaranteed delivery day.

## Two common methods

- from last menstrual period
- from known conception date

When the first day of the last period is known, calculators usually count forward about 40 weeks. When conception date is known, the estimate is adjusted from there. Both methods are reasonable starting points, but they depend on how accurate the dates are.

## Why confirmation still matters

Clinical dating and ultrasound information can refine the timeline beyond a simple calendar estimate. That is especially helpful when cycles are irregular, ovulation happened later than expected, or the last period date is uncertain.

## What the date is actually useful for

Most people do not need the due date because they expect birth on that exact day. They need it because it helps frame appointments, trimester timing, and the general pace of pregnancy.

That is why a due date works best as a planning anchor. It gives the next few months structure, even though the body may not follow the calendar exactly.
